Introduction

This research aims to draw conclusions and insights from the National Survey of Children's Health (NSCH) , a survey developed and administered each year by the U.S. Census Bureau with funding from the Health Resources and Services Administration (HRSA) Maternal and Child Health Bureau (MCHB) . This household survey collects national- and state- estimates of the physical and mental health of children 0-17 years old. All 50 states and the District of Columbia participate. The NSCH includes the following topics: Demographics for the children and families Children’s physical and mental health status Health insurance status, adequacy, and type of coverage Access to and use of health care services Early childhood-specific information (0-5 years) Middle childhood and adolescent-specific information (6-17 years) Family health and activities Parental perceptions of neighborhood and community characteristics Community-level data are available for participating areas, allowing them to compare their areas to their neighbors, other states and the nation. Local public health agencies, day-care centers and schools can use their community results to honestly evaluate their systems and processes, gaining insights into where improvements can be made. This survey also promotes inter-agency cooperation for reform and lasting change. This analysis is limited to state-level comparisons and national estimates, as identifiers at the local level are not available on the public database. Data from the NSCH are used for a variety of purposes and as such a large body of reports and peer-reviewed publications are available.

This project explores various machine learning models applied to data from the National Survey of Children's Health (NSCH). The dataset includes a wide range of variables related to children’s physical and mental health, family environment, and access to healthcare.

The primary goal is to build and compare different models for classification, regression, clustering, and dimensionality reduction to extract meaningful insights and make predictions about child health outcomes.

Techniques explored include:

  • Data Cleaning and Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)
  • Clustering (K-means, Hierarchical)
  • Dimensionality Reduction (PCA)
  • Classification (Naive Bayes, Decision Trees, SVM, Neural Networks)
  • Regression Analysis

All models are evaluated based on accuracy, interpretability, and their relevance to public health insights.
